In his last few years, my grandfather lived overcome by the confusion of his illness; the same illness so many of us know, and which we all fear.
The strength I once saw in his hands progressively gave way to increasingly feeble gestures; his ever-joyful spirit to the sorrowful gazes of his final days.

Yet, despite how hard this might be, I simply cannot remember my grandfather, my nonno, like this at all. 
Instead, my memories of him are those of the many Sunday mornings he would take my brother and me to pick up herbs for Nonna's sauce.
I remember of him as the man who showed my brother and I what the world was, what childhood was about---what it meant to be a child, through his own eyes of wonder.
I remember him playing with my sister, making her laugh, aloud, singing together with her, loving her, and showing her that good men exist.

This, this is how I remember my Nonno: a good man. Steady, secure, nothing but firm in his obligations.
I remember him as the man I saw getting up late at night and early in the morning, well past his seventies, to go to work.
I remember him entirely dedicated to asking nothing to nobody, to providing for himself, his caring wife, his two daughters, and the three grandchildren he had always had at home with him, and that he will continue to have home now that he's gone.

I remember my grandfather as the person who taught me, and I am sure many others, the values of family and hard work, all without ever even having to say a word, simply by showing us the example of a man working for his family.
I remember him as the man who showed me how to shave, and how to wear a tie.
I remember his way of teaching me and my brother how to shake hands with the world---with the firmness and the good-natured kindness he always possessed, and which is now ours to carry forward.
